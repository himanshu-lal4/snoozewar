## 1. Color Psychology for Premium

### What Colors Signal Premium/Luxury in Digital Products

**Low saturation = luxury.** A 2025 Journal of Consumer Research study ("The Color of Status," Zhou, Xiao, Yoon, Zhu) across 7 experimental and field studies found that **less saturated colors enhance consumers' perception of luxury brand status**. The mechanism: consumers associate desaturated colors with the passage of time, leading to perceptions of "continuity heritage" — which confers higher status. This aligns with the "quiet luxury" trend dominating premium branding.
- Source: [Journal of Consumer Research, 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/advance-article/doi/10.1093/jcr/ucaf029/8120421)

**Premium color palette hierarchy (digital):**
- **Black** — sophistication, authority, exclusivity
- **Deep navy/charcoal** — professional, premium without heaviness
- **Gold/amber accents** — wealth, luxury, divine association across most cultures
- **Deep jewel tones** (desaturated emerald, sapphire, amethyst) — premium positioning
- **Purple** — historically royalty/luxury; creativity, wisdom, mystery
- Source: [Smashing Magazine, 2025](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2025/08/psychology-color-ux-design-digital-products/)

**Critical finding: Context > inherent color associations.** Perceived appropriateness of color to brand/product is MORE influential than the color's inherent psychological association. A "discipline/enforcement" app needs colors that feel authoritative and energizing — not just generically "luxury."

### Dark Mode Psychology — Why Dark Feels Premium

**Dark themes evoke sophistication, exclusivity, and focus** — qualities that resonate with premium brands. Users perceive dark interfaces as **modern and professional**, not experimental.
- Source: [Almax Agency](https://almaxagency.com/design-trends/the-psychology-of-light-vs-dark-modes-in-ux-design/)

**Cognitive load reduction.** Darker surfaces reduce perceived visual noise, lowering cognitive load for scanning dashboards, reading short bursts of text, and viewing data visualizations — all core interactions in a score/streak-based app.
- Source: [Ergonomics Journal, 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00140139.2025.2483451)

**Dark mode triggers "focus state."** In a dark environment, bright accent elements command more attention. This is ideal for a Dare Engine UI where the challenge/dare card needs to pop against the background.

### Accent Colors on Dark Backgrounds

**Gold/amber on dark = universally premium.** The richness of gold against dark backgrounds creates depth and visual interest that "exudes sophistication." Amber excels at establishing clear visual hierarchy — it pops against both light and dark backgrounds.
- Source: [Figma Color Reference](https://www.figma.com/colors/amber/)

**Warm accents outperform cool on dark backgrounds for engagement:**
- Saffron/amber increases click-through rates by **12-18% in A/B tests** in wellness and financial apps where warmth signals trust
- Gold as an accent on buttons/icons gives "a touch of luxury and importance" — particularly effective for premium services
- Source: [Octet Design](https://octet.design/journal/luxury-colors/)

**Recommendation for SnoozeWar:** Primary accent of warm amber/gold (#F5A623 range) on deep dark gray (#121212 base). Reserve a secondary cool accent (desaturated teal or blue) for informational/progress elements. This creates warmth (motivation) + cool (data/trust) duality.

- Sources: [Color Psychology in Indian Fashion](https://www.cbazaar.com/blog/the-psychology-of-color-in-indian-fashion/), [App Growth Summit](https://appgrowthsummit.com/the-intersection-of-culture-and-design-a-comparative-of-japanese-and-western-apps/)

### How Many Colors Should a Premium App Use?

**3-5 colors total.** Most strong brands use at least 3-5 colors: one main color, two neutrals, and an accent color.
- Source: [Nine Blaess](https://www.nineblaess.de/blog/how-many-colours-should-a-brand-have/)

**The 60-30-10 rule:**
- 60% dominant (dark background — #121212 or similar dark gray)
- 30% secondary (lighter dark surfaces for cards/elevation — #1E1E1E to #2C2C2C)
- 10% accent (amber/gold for CTAs, highlights, active states)

**Real-world precedent:** Spotify uses black (60%) + dark gray (30%) + vibrant green (10%). This exact structure works for SnoozeWar with amber replacing green.
- Source: [UX Planet](https://uxplanet.org/the-60-30-10-rule-a-foolproof-way-to-choose-colors-for-your-ui-design-d15625e56d25)

### Gold/Amber vs Cool Tones for Premium

| Factor | Gold/Amber (Warm) | Blue/Teal (Cool) |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Premium perception | High — luxury, wealth, exclusivity | Moderate — trust, corporate, calm |
| Energy/motivation | High — stimulates enthusiasm and excitement | Low — calming, passive |
| Dark mode contrast | Excellent — pops naturally against dark | Good — but can feel clinical |
| Cultural safety | Universally positive across target markets | Safe but less premium |
| Emotional resonance | Warmth, achievement, reward | Reliability, logic, trust |

**Verdict:** Gold/amber as primary accent for premium + motivation. Cool desaturated teal/blue for secondary data elements (progress charts, informational text). This mirrors what top health apps do — warm for engagement, cool for data.

## 2. Typography Psychology

### What Font Characteristics Signal Premium

**Luxury typography characteristics (research-backed):**
- **Thin-to-medium weights** — refined, delicate, not bold/heavy (which signals urgency/cheap)
- **Increased letter-spacing** (tracking) — wide spacing creates "a sense of openness and luxury" with refined, spaced-out characters suggesting attention to detail
- **Generous line height** (1.5-1.7x for body) — breathing room = premium
- **Uppercase with wide tracking for headings** — signals authority and exclusivity (e.g., "YOUR DARE" in spaced caps)
- Source: [Cognition Today](https://cognitiontoday.com/font-psychology-research-and-application/), [Font Gem](https://www.fontgem.com/psychology-of-fonts)

**Serif vs sans-serif for mobile:**
- Serif fonts traditionally signal luxury, heritage, sophistication
- However, **major luxury brands are shifting to sans-serif** for digital (Burberry, Saint Laurent, Balenciaga all dropped serif logos)
- On mobile screens, sans-serif fonts outperform serif in readability tests
- Sans-serif stays sharp at small sizes where serifs blur
- **Recommendation:** Sans-serif for UI with optional serif for display/marketing text only
- Source: [Journal of Business and Administrative Research](https://www.sciedupress.com/journal/index.php/jbar/article/download/22993/14296)

### Letter-Spacing and Perceived Luxury

**Wider letter-spacing = perceived premium.** Research confirms that increased spacing improves fluency and signals "attention to detail and premium quality." Luxury brands consistently use wider tracking in their typography.

**Practical values:**
- Body text: +0.2px to +0.5px tracking (subtle, aids readability)
- Headings: +1px to +3px tracking (noticeable luxury signal)
- Uppercase labels/badges: +2px to +5px tracking (the "premium caps" look)
- Source: [Cognition Today](https://cognitiontoday.com/font-psychology-research-and-application/)

### Font Size Hierarchy for Premium Feel

Premium apps use **dramatic size contrast** between hierarchy levels:
- Display/hero: 32-48px (bold moment, used sparingly)
- H1 heading: 24-28px
- H2 subheading: 18-20px
- Body: 16px (never smaller on mobile)
- Caption/metadata: 12-14px (low opacity, not small AND dark)
- The gap between levels matters — premium = clear steps, not gradual

### How Many Fonts Should a Premium Product Use?

**Maximum 2 fonts.** One for headings, one for body. Using more than 2 creates visual noise that kills premium feel. Many top premium apps use a single font family with weight variation.

## 3. Spacing & Layout Psychology

### Whitespace and Perceived Value

**Direct correlation between whitespace and perceived value.** Consumer psychology shows a consistent correlation between generous, well-executed whitespace and perceptions of sophistication, trust, and premium quality — contributing directly to willingness to pay and brand loyalty.
- Source: [Liu & Li, SAGE Open, 2024](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/21582440241294129)

**Simple design = higher willingness to pay.** A field study (N=1,353) plus six preregistered studies found that simple packaging/design evokes "few-ingredients inferences" that increase perceived product purity and willingness to pay. The effect is driven by the inference that simplicity = focus = quality.
- Source: Same study, replicated across 6 studies

**Amazon's pixel test:** Amazon found that adding just **10 pixels of space** around product images generated a **1.8% revenue increase** across their entire marketplace. Spacing literally generates money.
- Source: [Web Design Swansea](https://websitedesignswansea.co.uk/2025/08/15/the-psychology-of-white-space-in-online-shopping/)

### How Spacing Affects Willingness to Pay

The research is clear: **more space = higher perceived value = higher WTP.**

| Design Approach | Perceived Value | Willingness to Pay |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Dense, cluttered | Low ("cheap marketplace") | Lower |
| Moderate spacing | Medium | Moderate |
| Generous whitespace | High ("premium boutique") | Higher |

**Mechanism:** Whitespace signals that the brand can "afford" to use space without cramming — the physical-world equivalent of a luxury store with few items on display vs. a dollar store crammed to the ceiling.

### Grid Systems That Feel Premium

- **8px grid system** — industry standard for premium mobile apps. All spacing, padding, margins are multiples of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48...)
- **Generous margins** — minimum 16px screen edge margins, 24px preferred for premium
- **Card spacing** — 12-16px between cards, never edge-to-edge without breathing room
- **Section spacing** — 32-48px between major content sections

### Information Density vs Premium Perception

**Low density = premium. High density = utility/cheap.**

This is culturally modulated:
- **Western markets (US, Europe):** Strong preference for minimal density. Clean layouts with generous whitespace = premium.
- **Japan:** Higher information density is expected and even preferred. "Fullness signals value and trust."
- **India:** Middle ground — trust signals and information are valued, but aspirational premium brands use Western-style whitespace.

**For a global app:** Default to Western-style minimal density (your primary markets are US/Europe) with information-dense sections available via progressive disclosure (satisfies Japan/India without cluttering the primary UI).
- Source: [App Growth Summit](https://appgrowthsummit.com/the-intersection-of-culture-and-design-a-comparative-of-japanese-and-western-apps/), [Andy Ng, Medium](https://andyngj.medium.com/designing-across-cultures-on-why-culture-shapes-ux-more-than-you-think-15-ux-insights-from-western-79c07bd4e4ba)

## 4. Animation & Motion Psychology

### Animation Speed and Perceived Quality

**200-500ms is the premium sweet spot.** Research shows:
- **100-200ms:** Quick feedback (button taps, toggles) — responsive, snappy
- **200-300ms:** Standard transitions on mobile (Material Design recommendation)
- **300-500ms:** Larger transitions (page changes, modal appearances) — smooth, deliberate
- **>500ms:** Feels sluggish — "animations start to feel like a real drag"
- Source: [NN/G](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/animation-duration/), [Material Design 3](https://m3.material.io/styles/motion/easing-and-duration)

**Counterintuitive finding:** With single-attention animations, participants perceived **shorter wait times with moderate-speed animations than with fast-speed ones** (Journal of Consumer Research, 2025). Moderate = feeling of intentional polish. Too fast = jarring/cheap.
- Source: [Journal of Consumer Research, 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/advance-article/doi/10.1093/jcr/ucaf037/8165440)

### What Motion Patterns Feel Expensive

**Premium motion characteristics:**
1. **Ease-in-out curves** (not linear) — elements accelerate and decelerate naturally, like physical objects
2. **Deceleration curves for entries** — elements arrive at full velocity and slowly decelerate to rest (feels intentional, not abrupt)
3. **Staggered animations** — elements animate in sequence, not all at once (e.g., list items appearing with 50ms delays between each)
4. **Spring physics** — subtle overshoot/bounce on interactive elements feels tactile and expensive (Apple's standard)
5. **Parallax/depth movement** — background and foreground moving at different rates creates perceived depth/quality
- Source: [Material Design](https://m1.material.io/motion/duration-easing.html)

**What feels cheap:**
- Linear movement (no easing) — robotic, unnatural
- Instant state changes with no transition — feels broken
- Overly bouncy/playful animations — feels like a children's app
- Animations that are all the same duration — no hierarchy

### Haptic Feedback and Premium Perception

**Haptics are a strong premium signal.** In usability tests, participants consistently described iPhone's Taptic Engine haptics as "clean" and "responsive" while Galaxy haptics were called "jarring" or "unnecessary."
- Source: [iFixit](https://www.ifixit.com/News/16768/apple-taptic-engine-haptic-feedback)

**Haptics reduce cognitive load** by providing reliable sensory confirmation of actions, and create "an element of satisfaction and delight" that increases user loyalty and app usage.

**Premium haptic implementation for SnoozeWar:**
- Light tap on button presses (UIImpactFeedbackGenerator.style.light)
- Medium impact on completing a dare/challenge
- Success pattern (3 ascending taps) on streak milestones
- Warning pattern on snooze penalty
- Heavy impact on alarm dismiss
- Source: [Hypersense Software](https://hypersense-software.com/blog/2024/07/15/haptic-technology-user-experience/)

### Micro-Interactions That Increase Perceived Value

**Apps with purposeful animation see up to 30% longer session durations** compared to static interfaces.
- Source: [Tandfonline, 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10447318.2026.2630289)

**High-impact micro-interactions:**
- Score counter animating up/down (not jumping)
- Streak flame that flickers/grows with consecutive days
- Life Score ring that fills smoothly with subtle glow
- Dare card that slides in with spring physics
- Button press states with subtle scale (0.97x) + haptic
- Celebration particles on milestone achievement
- Progress bars that ease-in-out, not linear fill

## 5. Visual Hierarchy for Premium

### Focal Points Per Screen

**One primary focal point per screen.** Premium apps ruthlessly prioritize. Maximum 2 focal points if they serve different purposes (e.g., a hero metric + one CTA).

**The "squint test":** If you squint at a screen and more than 2 things compete for attention, it's cluttered. Premium apps pass this test.

### Progressive Disclosure and Premium Feel

Progressive disclosure — revealing complexity layer by layer — is the single most important pattern for premium perception. It satisfies:
- **Western users** who expect minimal primary interfaces
- **Information-seeking users** who want depth on demand
- **Cognitive load theory** — fewer visible options = fewer decisions = less anxiety = higher perceived quality
- Source: [NN/G](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/progressive-disclosure/), [Interaction Design Foundation](https://ixdf.org/literature/topics/progressive-disclosure)

**Premium implementation:** Show the Life Score number prominently. Tap to reveal breakdown. Tap a category to see detailed history. Each layer adds detail without the previous layer feeling incomplete.

### The "Less Is More" Data

**Forbes reports 94% of first impressions are design-related** — and simpler, more visually appealing designs win.

**Google's research** on visual complexity and prototypicality found that **websites with low visual complexity and high prototypicality** (familiar layout patterns) were perceived as highly appealing. The effect is measurable within 50ms.
- Source: [Google Research](https://research.google/pubs/the-role-of-visual-complexity-and-prototypicality-regarding-first-impression-of-websites-working-towards-understanding-aesthetic-judgments/)

**The reduction principle:** Every element you remove from a screen increases the perceived importance (and perceived quality) of what remains. Premium is not about adding premium elements — it's about removing non-premium ones.

- Source: [Forms.app](https://forms.app/en/blog/dark-mode-statistics), [Yahoo Finance / Digital Silk](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/dark-mode-design-becomes-gen-112900313.html)

**For SnoozeWar:** Dark-first design is mandatory for the 18-35 target. 76-85% of this demographic already uses dark mode. Light mode should exist as an option but dark is the default and the premium experience.

### Dark Mode and Perceived App Quality/Value

**Dark themes are perceived as modern and professional**, not experimental. Dark UIs give a "premium, futuristic appearance" favored in tech and creative industries.
- Source: [Orfeostory](https://www.orfeostory.com/dark-mode-becomes-more-than-a-trend-and-they-psychology-behind-ui-choices/)

**Cognitive benefit for task accuracy:** For hard and medium complexity tasks, dark theme **consistently outperformed light theme in task accuracy.** For young adult groups, researchers observed **improved performance in dark mode.** This matters for an alarm/behavior app where users interact in low-light, just-woke-up conditions.
- Source: [ACM Eye Tracking Study, 2025](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3715669.3725879)

### Premium vs Cheap Dark Mode

**What makes dark mode feel PREMIUM:**
1. **Use #121212, not pure black (#000000)** — Google Material Design recommends dark gray because it's easier to see shadows and depth. Pure black = OLED "crushed blacks" that feel flat and lifeless.
2. **Elevation through lightness** — higher surfaces are lighter. Use 5 elevation levels with white overlays from 0% (base) to 16% (highest).
3. **Off-white text, not pure white** — use #E0E0E0 to #CCCCCC for body text. Pure white on dark creates excessive contrast that strains eyes and feels harsh.
4. **Google's opacity system:** 87% white for high-emphasis text, 60% for medium-emphasis, 38% for disabled text.
5. **Subtle borders/glows** instead of drop shadows for component separation.
6. **Frosted glass/blur effects** at 10-25% transparency for overlays.
7. **Tonal elevation** (Material Design 3) — elevated surfaces receive a semi-transparent overlay of the primary color (amber tint on surfaces in SnoozeWar's case).
- Source: [Material Design 3](https://m3.material.io/styles/elevation/applying-elevation), [Uxcel](https://uxcel.com/blog/12-principles-of-dark-mode-design-627)

**What makes dark mode feel CHEAP:**
1. Pure black backgrounds (#000000)
2. Pure white text (#FFFFFF) — too harsh
3. Neon/oversaturated accent colors on dark (screams "gamer aesthetic")
4. No elevation hierarchy — everything is the same darkness
5. Colored text on dark backgrounds without sufficient contrast
6. Drop shadows on dark (invisible or look wrong)
7. Identical light-mode assets/icons on dark background (wrong opacity, colors don't adapt)

### Dark-on-Dark Layering Technique

**Material Design's elevation overlay system:**

| Elevation (dp) | White Overlay Opacity | Use Case |
|----------------|----------------------|----------|
| 0dp | 0% | Base background |
| 1dp | 5% | Cards, sheets |
| 2dp | 7% | Buttons resting |
| 3dp | 8% | Buttons hovered |
| 4dp | 9% | App bars |
| 6dp | 11% | FABs, snackbars |
| 8dp | 12% | Navigation drawers, menus |
| 12dp | 14% | Dialogs |
| 16dp | 15% | Popovers |
| 24dp | 16% | Top-level modals |

- Source: [Material Design](https://m2.material.io/design/environment/elevation.html), [Google Codelabs](https://codelabs.developers.google.com/codelabs/design-material-darktheme)

### Light/Glow Effects in Dark Mode

**Glow effects are the dark mode equivalent of shadows in light mode.** Use them to:
- Create focal points (subtle amber glow behind the Life Score)
- Signal active/selected states
- Add perceived depth without heavy elevation
- Create "ambient lighting" feel that makes interfaces feel alive

**Implementation:** Use box-shadow/elevation with the accent color at very low opacity (5-15%) and large spread. The glow should be felt, not obviously seen.

## 7. What Cheap Design Looks Like (Anti-Patterns)

### Specific Design Choices That Make Apps Feel Cheap

1. **Too many colors** — More than 3-4 colors without clear hierarchy = chaotic/amateur
2. **Inconsistent spacing** — Different padding values throughout = unpolished
3. **Small touch targets** — Below 44x44pt = feels like a web page crammed into mobile
4. **Default system styling** — Unstyled toggles, default date pickers, stock icons = no brand investment
5. **Stock photos** — Especially generic "diverse group smiling" images
6. **Gradients done wrong** — Aggressive linear gradients across large surfaces (2012 aesthetic)
7. **Too many font weights/sizes** — More than 4-5 distinct text styles = visual noise
8. **Misaligned elements** — Off-grid text and images = amateur hour
9. **Information overload** — Cramming features onto one screen = "putrid" as one study describes
10. **Inconsistent iconography** — Mixed icon styles (some outlined, some filled, different weights)
- Source: [Toptal](https://www.toptal.com/designers/mobile/mobile-app-design-mistakes), [Cubicle Ninjas](https://cubicleninjas.com/impossibly-ugly-mobile-app-designs/)

### Common Dark Mode Mistakes

1. **Just inverting colors** — Light mode with inverted colors is NOT dark mode design
2. **Saturated colors that worked on light** — Must desaturate colors for dark backgrounds (200-level Material tones, not 500-level)
3. **Same illustrations/assets** — Light-mode illustrations look wrong on dark
4. **No elevation system** — Everything at the same level = flat, lifeless
5. **Using shadows** — Shadows are invisible on dark; use lighter surface + borders instead
6. **Pure black + pure white** — Maximum contrast is not premium, it's harsh

### Over-Decoration vs Under-Decoration

**Under-decoration wins for premium.** The research consistently shows:
- Fewer elements = higher perceived quality
- Simple packaging increases willingness to pay (replicated in 6 studies)
- Low visual complexity + familiar patterns = highest appeal ratings at 50ms

**The premium equation:** Fewer elements x Higher execution quality = Premium perception. One beautifully animated Life Score ring > five static metrics with decorative borders.

## 9. Cultural Premium Perception

### Does "Premium" Look Different Across Markets?

**Yes, significantly.** The core insight: **premium is defined by cultural context**, not universal aesthetics.

**United States / Western Europe:**
- Minimalism = premium. Clean layouts, generous whitespace, clear hierarchy.
- "Less is more" approach — negative space signals confidence
- Sans-serif typography, muted colors, high contrast imagery
- Source: [Raw.Studio](https://raw.studio/blog/how-cultural-differences-influence-ux-design/)

**India:**
- Trust signals are critical — badges, certifications, social proof
- Gold/saffron carries deep cultural premium associations (wealth, divinity, purity)
- Aspirational premium brands CAN use Western-style whitespace
- Cash-on-delivery preference reflects trust concerns that extend to digital design
- Vibrant colors are culturally expected but premium brands use restrained versions
- Source: [MoldStud](https://moldstud.com/articles/p-designing-mobile-apps-for-different-cultural-preferences)

**Japan:**
- Higher information density is preferred — "fullness signals value and trust"
- Meticulous attention to detail and micro-interactions = premium signal
- Wabi-sabi influence: subdued colors, natural/organic textures, understated elegance
- "Shibui/Shibumi" aesthetic — understated elegance, quiet refined taste, subdued palettes
- Black, deep navy, and natural earth tones carry premium associations
- Source: [UXmatters](https://www.uxmatters.com/mt/archives/2023/12/innovating-or-imitating-the-interplay-of-western-and-asian-digital-product-design.php)

### Strategy for Global Premium Design

**Use Western minimalism as the base** (primary US/Europe market), then:
1. **Progressive disclosure** for Japan — depth available on demand
2. **Gold/amber accent** for India — culturally resonant premium signal
3. **Trust signals** for India — streak badges, verification, social proof
4. **Quality micro-interactions** for Japan — the detail-oriented audience
5. **Dark mode base** works globally — sophistication reads across all markets

## 10. Data-Backed Design Rules

### First Impressions: The 50ms Rule

**Users judge quality in 50 milliseconds** — and that judgment rarely changes with more exposure time. 50ms → 500ms correlations are extremely high.
- Source: [Lindgaard et al., 2006, Behaviour & Information Technology](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01449290500330448)

**What determines the 50ms judgment:**
- Visual complexity (lower = better)
- Prototypicality (familiar patterns = better)
- Color harmony
- Spacing and alignment
- Overall "visual appeal" is the prime determiner
- Source: [Google Research](https://research.google/pubs/the-role-of-visual-complexity-and-prototypicality-regarding-first-impression-of-websites-working-towards-understanding-aesthetic-judgments/)

### The Stanford Credibility Finding

**46.1% of consumers** assessed site credibility based on visual design alone — layout, typography, font size, and color schemes. Design is the #1 signal of trustworthiness.
- Source: [Stanford Web Credibility Project](https://credibility.stanford.edu/)

### Color and Brand Recall

**Strategic color use boosts brand recall by up to 80%.** Among neuromarketing strategies, color psychology shows the strongest correlation with buying decisions.
- Source: [AMRA and ELMA](https://www.amraandelma.com/color-psychology-in-branding-statistics/)

### Animation Timing

```
instant:    100ms  (button press feedback, toggle)

Easing:
  standard:     cubic-bezier(0.4, 0.0, 0.2, 1)    (most transitions)
  decelerate:   cubic-bezier(0.0, 0.0, 0.2, 1)    (elements entering)
  accelerate:   cubic-bezier(0.4, 0.0, 1, 1)      (elements exiting)
  spring:       Custom spring(damping: 15, stiffness: 150) (interactive elements)
```

### The Premium Checklist

- [ ] Dark gray base (#0D-#14 range), never pure black
- [ ] Off-white text (87% opacity), never pure white
- [ ] Max 2 fonts, max 5 text size levels
- [ ] 60-30-10 color ratio (dark / surface / amber)
- [ ] 8px grid system, generous spacing
- [ ] 1 focal point per screen maximum
- [ ] Elevation through surface lightness, not shadows
- [ ] Custom data visualizations (no chart library defaults)
- [ ] Purposeful animation on every state change (200-400ms)
- [ ] Haptic feedback on all primary interactions
- [ ] Progressive disclosure for information depth
- [ ] Letter-spacing increased on headlines and labels
- [ ] Amber/gold accent for CTAs and premium moments
- [ ] Subtle glow effects for focal elements
- [ ] Staggered list animations (50ms delay between items)
- [ ] Spring physics on interactive elements
- [ ] No stock photos, no default system components
- [ ] All icons same style, weight, and optical size
- [ ] Screenshots optimized for App Store (test first screenshot)
- [ ] Pass the "squint test" — 1-2 focal points visible
